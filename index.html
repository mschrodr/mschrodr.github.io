Background on Wildfires

Fires are a natural occurrence on the landscape and can be both destructive and beneficial to the ecosystems it touches. By looking at charcoal data from North America it can been seen that fire frequency increased between 1750 and 1870 largely due to human population growth. But by the 20th century overall burning decreased, which is likely linked to agriculture expansion, livestock growth, and new fire prevention policies (Nature Geoscience, p697-702). By the 20th century immediate wildfire suppression become policy for US forest service. During 1935 the Forest service adapted its “10 AM Policy” in which all fires need to be under control by 10 o’clock of the morning following the wildfires discovery (Introduction to Wildland Fire, print). But around 1970 Fire Ecology research began to show how naturally caused fires are actually beneficial to forests and can also be an integral part of their further growth (Plants and Vegetation, p666). This in turn led to a complete change in wildfire policy, where fire suppression is reserved only for helping to save lives, property, or endangered wildlife and their habitat.

This led the forest service and forest firefighters to look into how fires move over different types of terrain features. Studies in fire ecology have begun to look at how the spatial pattern or arrangement of landscapes are often used as filters or places where distinctive pattern for fire (Land Mosaics, p22). A human aspect in fire behavior are its linear roads that run throughout primer wildfire habitats. In 2011 the International Journal of Wildland Fires published a study that was conducted to see what the influences forest road had on the spatial pattern of wildfires. It was concluded that when it comes to fires roads “tend to have the largest influence of any single variable, particularly in lower-elevation landscapes with relatively high road density” and that “roads themselves should be recognized as a unique type of fuel treatment.” (International Journal of Wildland Fire, yr. 2011, p792). Yet roads are only one out of many possible influences that effect wildfire habits and trends over a landscape.
